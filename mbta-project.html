MBTA Ridership Analysis (2018–2025)

A multi-layered analysis of Boston’s transit system, exploring systemwide trends, pandemic disruptions, event-driven ridership spikes, and long-term behavioral shifts.

Project Overview

I analyzed over six years of MBTA ridership data to understand how travel patterns in Boston changed across the pandemic, how major events like Red Sox home games affect station-level demand, and how different lines contribute to overall system throughput. I created a suite of static and interactive visualizations using Python and Vega-Lite to uncover patterns in recovery, peak-hour smoothing, and external drivers of ridership.

1. Systemwide Trends Across Pandemic Phases

This visualization breaks the timeline into three eras: pre-pandemic stability, a dramatic mid-pandemic collapse, and a gradual but incomplete recovery. Ridership fell by more than 80 percent in early 2020 and has since stabilized at about 70–80 percent of pre-pandemic levels.

2. Event-Driven Demand: Red Sox Home Games at Kenmore Station

Kenmore Station shows clear, predictable ridership spikes tied to Red Sox home games. During baseball season, monthly entries increase by 20–45 percent. This demonstrates how entertainment events create strong, measurable impacts on transit demand.

3. Station-Level Demand

An interactive ranking of the busiest MBTA stations shows that South Station, Downtown Crossing, and Harvard carry some of the highest daily gate entries. Red and Orange Line stations dominate the top tiers.

4. Line-Level Ridership Distribution

The Red Line accounts for the largest share of systemwide ridership, followed closely by the Orange Line. The Green Line is more diffuse due to its branches, while the Blue and Silver Lines form smaller but steady portions of demand.

5. Weather and Ridership Patterns

Weather conditions play a measurable role in daily travel decisions. Heavy rain reduces ridership at most stations, and extreme temperatures lead to predictable dips at outdoor-dependent transfer points.

6. Temporal Patterns: Ridership Heatmap

The ridership heatmap highlights the smoothing of peak travel hours post-pandemic. Hybrid work schedules shifted travel away from rigid rush hours, distributing demand more evenly across the day.

Technical Approach

  • Processed multi-year ridership datasets and normalized station IDs
  • Calculated monthly and daily averages, rolling windows, and seasonal comparisons
  • Joined rail ridership data with Red Sox home game schedules
  • Designed custom Matplotlib charts with annotations and multi-axis scaling
  • Built Vega-Lite interactive graphics for line, station, and weather patterns

Key Insights

  • Ridership plummeted by more than 80 percent during early 2020
  • System recovery plateaued at around 70–80 percent of pre-pandemic levels by 2024
  • Red Sox home games boosted Kenmore Station entries by 20–45 percent
  • Red and Orange Lines carry the majority of system throughput
  • Peak-hour demand is flatter post-pandemic, reflecting hybrid work adoption

What I Learned

This project strengthened my ability to structure large datasets, translate quantitative patterns into clear narratives, and design visuals that reveal meaningful behavior. It helped me understand how data-driven insights support transportation planning, product thinking, and operational decision-making.
